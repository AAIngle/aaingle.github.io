Outer Wilds
Image sourced from Outer Wilds Steam page

Synopsis

Play as an alien exploring their solar system to piece together a cosmic mystery. Fly your ship, traverse planets, translate ancient writings, and solve puzzles before time runs out.

Review

When I was first playing Outer Wilds, I couldn't stop thinking about it. The web of myserty is intricate and can be approached from any angle, the exploration and discovery are addicting, and the locations and characters are at once comforting and unlike anything else. It doesn't take long before the game gets its claws in you. And if the puzzles or planets aren't the first to do so, the music will probably be what gets stuck in your head. Even years later, the music makes me feel like I'm playing the game for the first time again. Which brings me to the first downside of Outer Wilds -- there's almost no chance of replayability. The puzzles aren't solved by getting items or gaining abilities; they're solved by how much you know about the story, which isn't easy to forget. There's really only one ending to the game, dialogue is limited and there isn't anything new to read once you've solved everything, and unless you haven't played the game's DLC yet, there's no post-game content or new game plus. The whole story takes maybe 20-30 hours but after that, the game has nothing left for you. It's a truly unique and touching few dozen hours but many players feel an Outer Wilds shaped hole in their hearts for a long time after.

This makes me biased, but my love for this game has grown like a pearl in my mind; the more I think about it, the bigger it gets. I think many games are fun while they last, but don't really stick with you after credits roll. Outer Wilds can't be more different than that. When I first reached the ending, it was actually a bit underwhelimg to me -- I didn't really understand what had happened. As I thought about it more for weeks, then months, then years, it has become one of the most important stories to me. The emotional impact is often what inspires people who have played Outer Wilds to convince others to play it themselves; someone in my life convinced me on that premise and I convinced others after I played it. Unfortunately, this adoration and pressure from Outer Wilds fans can sometimes intimidate other players. For as much as fans talk about this game, they don't talk about the actual content of the game for fear of ruining the surprise.

And I'll have to do the same here -- it might be annoying if you've heard it before, but this game is best enjoyed knowing as close to nothing about it as possible! If anything about time-loop puzzles, space exploration, or cosmic mysteries strikes your fancy, I can't recommend Outer Wilds enough.
